Part 2 · The chemistry

The five bonds that fold a protein

Folding is a tug-of-war between many weak interactions and a few strong covalent ones. Click a bond to learn where it acts — and note whether it lives on the backbone or the R-side-chain.

💡 Key idea: backbone bonds (peptide + the H-bonds of helices/sheets) build the local 2° scaffold. R-side-chain bonds (hydrophobic, ionic, H-bond, disulfide) fold that scaffold into the 3-D shape and lock chains together. Every side-chain bond depends on which amino acid the sequence supplied — no cysteine in the sequence, no disulfide possible.

Part 5 · The clinic

When folding goes wrong: 7 clinical cases

Each disease below is one broken link in the sequence → structure → function chain. Select a case to see the exact mutation, the bond or property it wrecks, and what shows up in your patient.

🩺 Connecting thread — rhabdomyolysis: several of these muscle diseases (porcine stress syndrome, PSSM1, PFK deficiency) converge on the same endpoint — rhabdomyolysis: muscle fibres over-contract or starve for energy, their membranes rupture, and myoglobin + creatine kinase spill into the blood (dark urine, high CK, possible kidney injury). A leaky Ca²⁺ channel, an over-active enzyme, and a missing glycolytic enzyme are chemically unrelated, yet the patient presents the same way. The chemistry diverges; the clinical picture converges.
